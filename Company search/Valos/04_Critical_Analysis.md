# Valos — Critical Analysis

## What Valos Gets Right

### 1. Founders Are Practitioners
Two of three co-founders (Kountourides and Davis) are MRICS Registered Valuers who worked at respected firms (Montagu Evans, Workman LLP). They built Valos to solve their own problem — manual report writing. This is the opposite of Finvise, where zero founders had valuation experience.

### 2. Product-Market Fit Is Real
Knight Frank, Colliers, Lambert Smith Hampton, Eddisons — these are serious firms. You don't win Knight Frank without a product that works. The client list validates that the report automation problem is real and Valos solves it well enough for enterprise adoption.

### 3. Valuer Remains in Control
Valos explicitly does NOT generate valuations. The platform automates data gathering and report compilation around the valuer's professional judgement. This respects the RICS framework — the valuer is the decision-maker, not a rubber stamp for an algorithm.

### 4. Clean Corporate Governance
No director departures, no late filings, no offshore PSCs, founders retain majority control. This is a well-run company by startup standards.

### 5. Capital Efficiency
£500k funding, no evidence of further raises, yet they've won major enterprise clients. Either they're generating meaningful revenue or they're extremely lean — both are good signals.

## What Valos Gets Wrong (or Doesn't Do)

### 1. It's a Report-Writing Tool, Not a Valuation Platform
Valos automates the **output** (the report) but does not enhance the **input** (the valuation judgement). It makes the existing process faster but doesn't make it smarter. The valuer still does all the analytical thinking — Valos just types it up faster.

This is the critical distinction: **Valos solves speed. PropVal solves intelligence.**

### 2. No AVM or Price Prediction Capability
Valos has no stochastic modelling, no ensemble methods, no market signal detection, no borough-separated analytics. They don't attempt to predict property values or provide valuation intelligence. Their "22% more accurate" claim refers to data accuracy in reports, not valuation accuracy.

### 3. NLP/NLG Technology Claims Are Vague
They claim to use "NLP/NLG" for report generation but provide no technical detail on the underlying models. In 2024-25, this likely means some form of LLM integration, but the specifics are opaque. How good is the generated text? Does it require heavy editing? These questions are unanswered publicly.

### 4. No UPRN Data Spine
There's no evidence of a persistent property-level data architecture. Valos appears to query data sources per-report, not accumulate intelligence per-property over time. Each report is a standalone event, not a contribution to a growing property intelligence library.

### 5. Modest Scale for the Client List
With ~10-15 employees and £500k total funding, they're serving Knight Frank and Colliers — firms with hundreds of valuers. This raises questions about implementation depth. Are these firm-wide rollouts or departmental pilots? The Eddisons press release mentions their "100-person valuation team" — if Valos is genuinely embedded across that team, the product is proven at scale. If it's a 5-person pilot, the logos overstate traction.

### 6. No Evidence of Data Moat
Valos aggregates publicly available data (OS, EA, VOA, etc.) — the same sources anyone can access. They don't appear to generate proprietary data, build comparable evidence libraries, or create network effects from usage. The switching cost is the workflow integration, not the data.

### 7. Single Investor, Now Deceased
Nigel Henry was their only named investor and board-level supporter. His death in February 2026 removes their most senior non-executive voice and potentially creates uncertainty around his shareholding (inheritance, estate decisions).

## Competitive Vulnerabilities

### 1. Replaceable by AI
Report automation is precisely the kind of task that general-purpose AI (Claude, GPT-4) can now do well. A surveyor with access to the same data sources and a good LLM prompt could replicate much of what Valos does without a subscription. Valos's moat is the packaged integration — but that moat is narrowing fast.

### 2. No Valuation Intelligence = No Lock-In
If a competitor offers both report automation AND valuation intelligence (price prediction, comparable analysis, market signals), firms would switch. Valos's product is necessary but not sufficient for the future of valuation tech.

### 3. Commercial Property Focus Limits TAM
Much of Valos's client base and marketing targets commercial valuation (Knight Frank, Colliers, Lambert Smith Hampton are primarily commercial firms). The residential valuation market — where volume is highest — may not be their primary focus. This leaves an opening.

### 4. RICS Technology Partner ≠ RICS Regulated
Valos is a "RICS Technology Partner" — a marketing relationship, not a regulatory status. They are not a RICS-regulated firm and cannot issue valuations. This is appropriate for their business model (they sell tools, not valuations), but it means they have no regulatory moat.

### 5. Pricing Unknown
No public pricing information. This makes competitive positioning difficult to assess, but also suggests bespoke enterprise pricing — which means sales cycles are long and CAC is high.

## The Nigel Henry Question

Nigel Henry's death (February 2026) creates several unknowns:
- **Board seat:** Does someone from Fusion Group take his seat, or does it remain vacant?
- **Shares:** His estate presumably inherits his stake. Will the estate remain a passive holder, or seek to sell?
- **Strategic guidance:** Henry brought property development experience and a network. That's now gone.
- **Future funding:** If Valos needs to raise again, they've lost their most connected board member.

This is not fatal — the company is founder-led and the product works — but it removes a layer of credibility and support at a critical growth stage.
