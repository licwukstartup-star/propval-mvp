# Finvise — Critical Analysis

## Fundamental Product Flaw

**The valuer is a rubber stamp, not a decision-maker.**

Finvise's model: AVM generates value → valuer signs it off → report goes to lender.

This inverts the professional relationship. The valuer carries PI liability, RICS accountability, and reputational risk — but has no real agency in the valuation judgement. "The algorithm told me" is not a defence before RICS disciplinary panels or in court.

The "2 second" valuation claim makes this explicit. No human valuer exercises professional judgement in 2 seconds.

## Accuracy Concerns

- **±10% accuracy in 95% of cases** — means 1 in 20 valuations could be wildly off
- **85%+ accuracy** (third-party listing) — 15% error rate is a negligence claim in professional valuation
- For RICS Red Book purposes, this level of accuracy is inadequate for lending decisions
- No published methodology for how "accuracy" is measured or validated independently

## RICS Regulation as a Shell

- **David Hunt, MRICS** is named as Partner/Director and "Head of Regulation and Compliance"
- In reality: part-time consultant, main job is academia/professor, occasionally attends meetings
- The RICS firm regulation exists on paper through David's credentials
- Founders (none MRICS) make all product decisions
- If David left, RICS regulation would be in jeopardy
- Spirit of RICS regulation = professional control over valuation process. Finvise uses it as a marketing badge.

## Operational Issues (Firsthand Observations)

1. **Outsourced development** — code built by contracted developer, expensive and slow
2. **Excessive spending** — office rent, premature salesman hires, more valuers before product was right
3. **Too many meetings** — 4 co-founders + employees = communication overhead, politics, slow decisions
4. **Premature valuer hiring** — hired more valuers creating politics and cost without fixing the core product
5. **No equity for key staff** — first RICS valuer (who made the business legally viable) was never offered shares, revealing how founders viewed valuers: as a cost, not a partner

## Competitive Vulnerability

1. **Regulatory fragility** — RICS status depends on employed/consultant surveyors, not founders
2. **Accuracy inadequate** — ±10% is not competitive for professional valuation
3. **No domain expertise at leadership level** — product decisions made by non-valuers
4. **Original vision holder departed** — remaining team executing someone else's vision
5. **Modest funding** — $1M seed is thin for the claims being made (global expansion, 11-50 employees)
6. **Offshore control** — UAE entity holding PSC raises governance questions for a RICS-regulated firm

## PI Liability Chain (Risk Scenario)

If a lender relies on a Finvise valuation that goes wrong:
1. Who signed this report? → One of the employed valuers
2. Who oversees the valuation process? → David Hunt, Head of Regulation
3. Where was David? → At his university, occasionally attends meetings
4. Who actually determined the value? → The AVM algorithm
5. Who built the algorithm? → A physics PhD and a maths education professor
6. Who had professional oversight of the methodology? → **Gap**

That gap is where PI claims and RICS disciplinary action lives.
